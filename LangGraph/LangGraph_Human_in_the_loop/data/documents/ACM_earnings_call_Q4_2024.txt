SARAH OKONKWO: Good morning, and welcome to ACME Industrial's fourth quarter and full
year 2024 earnings call. Before we begin, I'd like to remind everyone that this call
may contain forward-looking statements. Actual results may differ materially from those
projected. A reconciliation of non-GAAP measures is available in our press release.
I'll now turn the call over to our CEO, Margaret Holloway.

MARGARET HOLLOWAY (CEO): Thank you, Sarah, and good morning, everyone. Fiscal 2024 was
undeniably a difficult year for ACME. We faced a confluence of headwinds — weakening
industrial demand across North America and Europe, elevated raw material costs that
pressured our margins, and the operational disruption of launching Project Streamline
while maintaining customer commitments. Full-year revenue declined 8.1% to $2.4 billion,
and adjusted EBITDA came in at $374 million, well below our original guidance of
$420–440 million.

I want to be direct with investors: we are not satisfied with these results, and we
have made significant changes to address them. First, Project Streamline is on track.
We closed the Canton, Ohio facility in November and expect full run-rate savings of
$65 million by Q3 2025. Second, the divestiture of our Specialty Coatings division,
announced in January, will generate $87 million in cash proceeds to reduce debt.
Third, we have extended conversations with our lending syndicate about the $185 million
term loan maturity. We expect to announce a refinancing solution in Q1 2025.

Despite the challenges, our Aerospace & Defense segment was a bright spot, growing 14%
year-over-year and now representing 22% of total revenue. This is our highest-margin
segment at approximately 18% EBIT margin, and we see continued growth driven by
defense spending and commercial aviation recovery.

DAVID PARK (CFO): Thank you, Margaret. Let me walk through the Q4 and full-year
financials in more detail.

Q4 2024 revenue was $574 million, down 11.2% year-over-year and below our guidance of
$590–610 million. The shortfall was driven by a $24 million reduction in orders from
two large automotive customers who delayed their capex programs into 2025.

Full-year gross margin was 24.7%, a 320-basis-point compression from 27.9% in 2023.
Approximately 180 basis points of this compression was due to steel and aluminum cost
inflation, with the remaining 140 basis points from the lower fixed-cost absorption as
volumes declined.

Our balance sheet remains a priority focus. Total debt at year-end was $1.727 billion,
with net leverage of 4.6x trailing adjusted EBITDA of $374 million. Our credit agreement
allows up to 5.0x, so we have a 40-basis-point cushion. The team is laser-focused on
free cash flow conversion — we generated $201 million in operating cash flow in 2024,
and we are targeting $260–280 million in 2025 as restructuring charges normalize.

For 2025 guidance, we are projecting full-year revenue of $2.35–2.45 billion and
adjusted EBITDA of $390–420 million. We expect modest sequential improvement each
quarter as Project Streamline savings ramp and automotive customer demand recovers.

MARGARET HOLLOWAY (CEO): I want to close by acknowledging the concern we have heard from
shareholders regarding our near-term liquidity. We hear you. The refinancing of the
$185 million maturity is our single highest priority, and we expect to close that
transaction before the end of Q1 2025. We have strong relationships with our banking
partners, and preliminary indications are constructive.

ANALYST (Goldman Sachs): Margaret, you mentioned the term loan refinancing is your top
priority. Can you give us any more color — are you looking at a full refinancing or a
maturity extension, and what rate environment are you pricing against?

MARGARET HOLLOWAY: We're evaluating both options — an amendment and extension of the
existing facility, or a new term loan. Given current SOFR levels we expect the
all-in cost to increase by approximately 75–100 basis points versus our current rate,
which David can model into your interest expense estimate. We'll have more clarity in
the next 30 days.

ANALYST (JPMorgan): David, the covenant cushion of 40 basis points seems thin. What's
the plan if EBITDA comes in below the low end of guidance?

DAVID PARK: We have a covenant relief mechanism that we negotiated with our lending
group in Q3 2024 that allows us to add back up to $30 million in restructuring charges
for the purpose of covenant calculations. Even in a downside scenario where EBITDA came
in 10% below guidance, we would remain in compliance. We also have the $87 million
Coatings divestiture proceeds available to reduce debt and improve the leverage ratio.

ANALYST (BofA Securities): Can you comment on the Aerospace & Defense growth outlook?
Is the 14% growth rate sustainable?

MARGARET HOLLOWAY: We are very encouraged by the pipeline. We have $1.2 billion in
qualified opportunities in A&D, and our win rate has improved meaningfully. I would
characterize 10–14% organic growth as a realistic range for the next two to three years,
driven by both new program wins and aftermarket parts demand as the installed base grows.
This is the segment where we are selectively investing even as we cut costs elsewhere.
